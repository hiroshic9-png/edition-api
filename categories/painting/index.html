From the monochrome ink of Zen masters to the golden splendor of Momoyama screens, Japanese painting encompasses a vast range of expression — always grounded in the primacy of the brushstroke.

From Ink to Gold

Japanese painting encompasses an extraordinary range — from the monochrome ink landscapes (suiboku-ga) of Zen masters like Sesshū Tōyō to the lavish gold-leaf screens of the Kanō school, and the naturalistic beauty of the Rinpa tradition founded by Hon'ami Kōetsu and Tawaraya Sōtatsu.

The formats are themselves an art: hanging scrolls (kakemono) designed for seasonal rotation in the tokonoma alcove, hand scrolls (emakimono) that unfold narrative scenes horizontally, and massive folding screens (byōbu) that define architectural space.

Schools and Lineages

Understanding Japanese painting requires knowledge of its school system (ryūha). The Kanō school dominated official painting for over 400 years, serving shōguns and daimyō. The Tosa school preserved the yamato-e tradition of native Japanese subjects. The Rimpa school broke from both, creating bold decorative works that merged painting with craft.

Each school transmitted specific techniques through master-apprentice relationships, and their stylistic DNA — brushwork rhythm, compositional formulas, palette preferences — forms the basis for authentication analysis.

Reading a Hanging Scroll

A kakemono (hanging scroll) is a complete work of art — painting, calligraphy, mounting, and storage box form an integrated whole.

The mounting (hyōsō) itself communicates period, status, and intended context. Formal mountings (shin) use rich brocade and wide borders; semi-formal (gyō) blend elegance with restraint; and informal (sō) use simple fabrics suited to tea ceremony display. The ichimonji strips — narrow bands of gold brocade framing the painting — indicate the mounting's quality tier.

The jikusaki (roller ends) at the bottom are diagnostic markers: ivory for formal works, lacquer for semi-formal, plain wood for tea ceremony scrolls. The material, shape, and patina of the jikusaki can help date a mounting independently of the painting.

Hakogaki — The World Within the Box

The storage box (tomobako) of a Japanese painting carries inscriptions (hakogaki) that constitute a parallel provenance document. A hakogaki by the artist himself (jiga-jisan) dramatically increases a work's value and authenticity.

Successive owners, tea masters, and connoisseurs may add their own inscriptions to the box lid, creating a layered history of appreciation. The calligraphic style, ink quality, and seal impressions on these inscriptions are themselves subject to authentication analysis.

The box construction — paulownia (kiri) wood, fit without nails, with specific joint types — follows conventions that evolved over centuries. A mismatched box (the painting and box from different periods) is not unusual but requires explanation.
