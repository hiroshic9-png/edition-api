Lacquerware & Maki-e
漆器・蒔絵

EDITION › Categories › Lacquerware & Maki-e
In English, 'japan' once meant lacquerware — so completely was the craft identified with its origin. Maki-e, the art of sprinkling gold and silver powder onto wet lacquer, represents perhaps the most refined decorative technique ever devised.

Layers of Perfection

Japanese lacquer (urushi) is among the most labor-intensive art forms ever developed. A single piece may require thirty or more individual coats, each applied, dried in controlled humidity, and polished before the next. The finest maki-e (sprinkled picture) works incorporate gold and silver powder in multiple particle sizes, creating depth effects that appear to glow from within.

The art reached its apex during the Edo period, when inrō (medicine containers), suzuribako (writing boxes), and jubako (tiered food boxes) became canvases for extraordinary decorative expression.

The Inrō Tradition

Inrō — the nested medicine cases suspended from the obi sash — represent perhaps the most concentrated form of Japanese decorative art. Masters like Ogawa Haritsu (Ritsuō) and the Kajikawa family created pieces where every surface, including the interior, displays consummate skill.

Authentication of lacquerware is uniquely challenging: the techniques for creating artificial aging (nureba aging) have been refined over centuries. CT scanning reveals the layer structure — genuine Edo-period pieces show characteristic variations in lacquer thickness that machine-applied modern copies lack.

The Maki-e Hierarchy

Maki-e ('sprinkled picture') encompasses a hierarchy of techniques, each more demanding than the last.

Hiramaki-e (flat maki-e) — Gold or silver powder sprinkled onto wet lacquer, then sealed with transparent lacquer and polished flat. The foundation technique.

Togidashi maki-e (burnished-out maki-e) — The design is buried under layers of lacquer, then carefully polished back until the metal powder emerges flush with the surface, creating an effect of extraordinary subtlety.

Takamaki-e (raised maki-e) — The design is built up in relief using lacquer mixed with charcoal powder or clay, then decorated with metal powders. This creates a three-dimensional effect.

Shishiai-togidashi (combined techniques) — The pinnacle: raised, flat, and burnished techniques combined in a single work, creating landscapes with genuine depth and atmosphere. Only the most accomplished artists attempt this.

Wajima and Regional Traditions

Japan's lacquer traditions are geographically diverse, each region contributing distinctive methods and aesthetics.

Wajima-nuri (Ishikawa) — Considered the finest everyday lacquerware, using a ground (ji) reinforced with powdered diatomaceous earth (jinoko) that creates exceptional durability. Genuine Wajima ware can be identified by its distinctive ground layer under microscopic examination.

Tsugaru-nuri (Aomori) — Known for its unique marbled patterns created by layering and polishing multiple colors of lacquer.

Kamakura-bori (Kanagawa) — Carved wooden forms coated in layers of red and black lacquer, creating a sculptural approach to the medium.

The urushi tree itself is becoming scarce in Japan. Domestic lacquer (Japanese urushi) commands premium prices over Chinese imports, and its chemical composition — detectable through FTIR analysis — serves as a quality and origin marker.
